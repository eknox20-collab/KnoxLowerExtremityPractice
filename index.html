Body Composition
Body composition describes the proportion of fat mass vs. lean mass in the human body. Understanding it is essential for health, performance, and athletic training.
WHAT IS BODY COMPOSITION?

Body composition is the breakdown of what your body is made of, primarily fat mass and fat-free mass (lean mass). Unlike body weight alone, it gives a more accurate picture of health and fitness.

FAT MASS
Essential + storage fat
LEAN MASS
Muscle, bone, organs, water
MEASUREMENT METHODS — Click to Explore
Bod Pod
Uses air displacement plethysmography. Highly accurate, non-invasive, and quick. Subject sits in a sealed chamber and displaced air measures body volume and density.
Skinfold Calipers
Measures subcutaneous fat thickness at specific body sites. Values are plugged into prediction equations. Low cost and portable but accuracy depends on technician skill.
BIA
Bio-Impedance Analysis sends a harmless electrical current through the body. Fat impedes the current more than lean tissue. Hydration levels significantly affect results.
Hydrostatic Weighing
The gold standard. Subjects are weighed underwater. Fat is less dense than water and muscle is denser, enabling precise body density calculation.
Emerging Technologies
Ultrasound measures fat thickness and muscle cross-section. DXA scans use low-dose X-ray to differentiate fat, lean mass, and bone with excellent accuracy.
SAFE WEIGHT LOSS & GAIN PARAMETERS
Safe Weight Loss
  • 0.5 to 2 lbs per week maximum
  • 300 to 500 calorie daily deficit
  • Preserve lean muscle through resistance training
  • Maintain adequate protein intake
  • Avoid crash diets due to metabolic adaptation risk
Safe Weight Gain
  • 0.25 to 0.5 lbs per week for lean gains
  • 200 to 500 calorie daily surplus
  • Prioritize resistance training
  • Increased protein to support hypertrophy
  • Use a gradual approach to avoid excess fat gain

Nutritional Disorders
Disordered eating affects athletes at all levels. Recognizing signs and symptoms is a critical skill in sports medicine. Click each condition to expand.
Anorexia Nervosa
▾
Restrictive EatingBody Dysmorphia
SIGNS & SYMPTOMS
  • Severely restricted caloric intake
  • Intense fear of weight gain
  • Distorted body image
  • BMI below 17.5
  • Lanugo, brittle nails/hair, amenorrhea
EFFECTS
  • Bone density loss
  • Muscle atrophy and weakness
  • Cardiac arrhythmias
  • Electrolyte imbalances
  • Organ failure in severe cases
Bulimia Nervosa
▾
Binge-Purge CycleCompensatory Behavior
SIGNS & SYMPTOMS
  • Recurrent binge eating episodes
  • Self-induced vomiting or laxative misuse
  • Russell sign (knuckle calluses)
  • Swollen parotid glands
  • Tooth enamel erosion
EFFECTS
  • Electrolyte imbalances (hypokalemia)
  • Esophageal tears
  • Dehydration
  • Depression and anxiety
  • Often normal weight, hard to detect
Muscle Dysmorphia
▾
Body Dysmorphic DisorderMore common in males
SIGNS & SYMPTOMS
  • Obsessive preoccupation with being too small
  • Compulsive weightlifting and steroid abuse
  • Extreme dietary control
  • Distress when unable to exercise
EFFECTS
  • Overtraining and overuse injuries
  • Social isolation
  • Hormone disruption from steroid use
  • Cardiac risks and depression

PROPHYLACTIC TAPING TERMINOLOGY
Anchor
Starting strip applied proximal or distal to the area. Holds subsequent strips in place.
Stirrup
U-shaped strip running under the heel from medial to lateral malleolus, providing inversion and eversion support.
Horseshoe
Strip applied around the malleolus in a U-shape to stabilize and protect bony prominences.
Spica
Figure-eight wrapping technique used for thumb, great toe, or hip to limit range of motion.
Heel-lock
Strips anchored at the heel crossing around it to prevent excessive inversion and stabilize the calcaneus.
Checkrein / Fan
Fan-shaped strips limiting excessive extension or flexion, used for finger and wrist injuries.

Inversion / Eversion
Inversion: sole turns inward. Eversion: sole turns outward. Most ankle sprains involve inversion.
Dorsiflexion / Plantarflexion
Dorsiflexion: toes toward the shin. Plantarflexion: toes point downward like pressing a gas pedal.
